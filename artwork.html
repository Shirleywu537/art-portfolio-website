    Welcome!

    My artwork has always been a place where I felt most grounded. Whether I was sketching in the back of a class notebook or experimenting with layering digital textures, I found comfort in creating. Art has helped me process emotions, tell stories, and stay curious. I didn’t start with any formal goals in mind, just a need to draw what I saw and sometimes what I didn’t.

    Over time, I began exploring different media and trying new approaches. I leaned into traditional techniques, then slowly introduced digital ones. I took inspiration from animated films, manga, architecture, and even quiet moments from daily life. Most of all, I loved watching an image come together, seeing it evolve, and realizing it could speak for itself.

    Charcoal

    Charcoal was probably the first medium that really forced me to slow down. I had to learn how to let go of control and allow the shadows to shape themselves. It was messy, unpredictable, and completely different from the neat sketching I was used to. I remember working on a still life of old fruit, smudging and lifting for hours, until it finally started to feel alive.

    Some of these pieces were shown in school art events and one was even selected for a local black and white gallery. But honestly, what I loved most was how charcoal let me explore memory and atmosphere. Even now, I still go back to it when I want to quiet everything else and just focus on light.

    Digital Art

    Digital art gave me so much freedom. I started out just experimenting, trying every brush and effect I could find in Procreate. Eventually, I developed a style that blended soft textures with bolder outlines. I loved playing with color the most—choosing combinations that felt slightly off but still made emotional sense.

    I’ve used digital art for comics, concept pieces, and visual poems. One project I’m especially proud of was a short digital illustration series about things left unsaid. I posted it without any captions, but people commented with their own interpretations, and that meant more to me than any award could.

    Drawing

    Drawing has always been the foundation of everything I make. I filled dozens of sketchbooks with anatomy studies, imagined characters, and scenes I saw in my head while listening to music. Most of these drawings never became finished pieces, but they taught me how to see. I would spend hours just sketching people walking by or reimagining old myths in pencil form.

    I submitted a few of these drawings to contests and portfolios, but what mattered most to me was how they helped me grow. Drawing was the space where I figured things out—composition, balance, perspective, and sometimes even how I felt.

    Illustration for School Publications

    I started illustrating for The Campanile, our school newspaper, in ninth grade. I remember being really nervous the first time—my drawing was supposed to represent something serious, and I worried I wouldn’t get it right. Since then, I’ve illustrated everything from student burnout to profiles on local artists. I learned to work under tight deadlines and turn abstract ideas into visuals that could support the story without overpowering it.

    One of my favorite pieces was a cover illustration that blended architecture and emotion to represent a sense of community. It took me three all-nighters, but I would do it again in a heartbeat. Working with the editorial team helped me think visually in a different way and taught me how to communicate with clarity and purpose.

    Mixed Media

    I began exploring mixed media because I wanted to make pieces that felt more physical. I loved the texture of stitching thread into paper, layering torn bits of old homework with watercolor stains, or using pressed flowers as part of the composition. These works weren’t about precision—they were about emotion, texture, and letting the materials guide me.

    One piece I remember used three languages overlaid in ink, thread, and paint to explore family memory. It wasn’t polished, but it felt very real. Working this way allowed me to tell stories that didn’t always fit into neat boxes, and that’s what made it so satisfying.

    Painting

    Painting gave me the chance to loosen up. With pencil, I was often cautious, but with a brush, I felt more instinctive. I experimented with acrylics, watercolors, and sometimes both at once. I painted places I missed, people I imagined, and small scenes that felt like moments in a dream.

    One series I worked on came from fictional diary entries. Each painting was based on a line or mood from something I had written in my journal. It became a way for me to reflect without words. I learned how to layer emotion into color and let the strokes speak for themselves.

    Sketches

    My sketchbooks are where everything begins. They’re filled with half-finished creatures, quick gesture drawings, fragments of poetry, and strange ideas I haven’t figured out yet. I try to sketch something every day, even if it’s just a tiny doodle. It keeps me connected to the creative part of myself, especially on days when everything else feels off.

    Some of my best ideas started as scribbles. I love flipping through old pages and watching my style shift and return in new forms. Sketching reminds me that progress isn’t always linear, and that roughness can hold its own kind of beauty.
